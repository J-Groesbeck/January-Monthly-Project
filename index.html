  • "Robinson Crusoe" is a novel written by Daniel Defoe, first published on April 25, 1719.
  • The novel is considered one of the earliest examples of realistic fiction and is often regarded as the first English novel.
  • It tells the story of Robinson Crusoe, a mariner who is shipwrecked and stranded on a deserted island for 28 years.
  • Crusoe's experiences on the island, including his struggle for survival, encounters with cannibals, and eventual companionship with Friday, make up the core of the narrative.
  • The novel is structured as a fictional autobiography, narrated by Robinson Crusoe himself.
  • "Robinson Crusoe" is known for its exploration of themes such as human resilience, self-reliance, and the consequences of colonialism.
  • The novel has been widely translated and adapted into various forms of literature, theater, and film, making it a classic in world literature.
  • Daniel Defoe's work laid the foundation for the development of the novel as a literary form and has had a lasting impact on storytelling.
